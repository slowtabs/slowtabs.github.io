# Six Months of Writing Publicly: What I Learned

An honest look at what's surprised me about maintaining a technical blog — the feedback loops, the drafts that never shipped, and the ones that did.

## I started for the wrong reasons

I started this blog because everyone says you should "build in public" and "write to think." Both true, but they weren't *my* reasons. I started because I felt like I should.

It took about two months before I found my actual reason: **writing forces me to finish thinking.** I have dozens of half-formed opinions about software. Writing is the only thing that makes me complete them.

## What surprised me

### The posts I was proud of flopped

My carefully crafted, week-long deep dive into event sourcing patterns? 47 views. My quick TIL about a TypeScript operator written in 20 minutes? 2,400 views.

I've stopped trying to predict what resonates. I write what interests me and let the internet decide.

### The feedback is mostly private

I expected comments and Twitter discourse. Instead, I get DMs. "Hey, I read your post about X, it helped me at work." These happen once or twice a month and they're the best part.

### Writing regularly is harder than I expected

I aimed for weekly. I've averaged bi-weekly. Some months I wrote four posts. Some months, zero. The guilt of missing a self-imposed deadline is real, even when no one else notices.

## What I'd tell someone starting today

1. **Write about what you just learned.** The best blog posts come from fresh confusion — when you still remember what was hard and can explain it without expert blind spots.

2. **Don't aim for comprehensive.** A 500-word post that explains one thing clearly is more useful than a 3,000-word post that explains everything poorly.

3. **Publish before you're ready.** Every post I've published was "not quite done." Hitting publish is a skill you have to practice.

4. **Build the habit before the audience.** If you write consistently for six months, an audience will find you. It won't be huge, but it'll be real.

5. **Keep a drafts folder.** Not every idea becomes a post. I have 15 abandoned drafts. That's fine — they're thinking exercises, not failures.

## The drafts that never shipped

- "Why I switched from VS Code to Neovim" (switched back after two weeks)
- "A complete guide to Docker networking" (realized I don't understand Docker networking)
- "Hot takes on the JavaScript ecosystem" (too spicy, not constructive)

I'm oddly fond of these. They represent curiosity that didn't become conviction, and that's okay.

## Six months in

I'm going to keep writing. Not because of metrics or "personal brand" or any of that. I'm going to keep writing because I think more clearly when I do, and the few people who read these posts occasionally tell me it helped them.

That's enough.
